Sag A* Flare Table


List of all Chandra observations on Sgr A* performed between 1999 and 2019. We collect results from Jacquet, Robeyns (2020), Bouffard et al. (2019), Mossoux et al. (2017), Ponti et al. (2015), and Neilsen et al. (2013). For each observation, the observation ID, observation start date, the instrument set-up, the exposure time, and the number of flares or flaring blocks (for Ponti et al.) are reported. In the following columns, we report the quiescent (non-flaring) rate, the flare start and stop times, the flare duration, the mean observed count rate, the peak count rate, the emitted energy, the flare flux, flare fluence, and flare luminosity. Authors may analyze data differently or report different flare properties, such that some columns are used only by some authors.

For all observations analyzed by Jacquet, Robeyns visit this Github repository to see the corresponding event files and light curves. All other event files and light curves were generated by Bouffard et al. and are available at this Github repository.

Bouffard et al. report pile-up corrected flare mean count rates that are not quiescence-subtracted. These values may be inaccurate for short flares with durations less than or comparable to a 300 s bin size. The 2-10 keV energies are pile-up corrected and quiescence-subtracted. Note that flares before 2012 reported to be from Bouffard et al. (2019) are not in that paper but were obtained using the same method. All values are reported for NH = 14.3 × 1022 cm−2 and Γ = 2.

Mossoux et al. report quiescence-subtracted flare mean count rates. The mean unabsorbed flux between 2-10 keV is determined for NH = 14.3 × 1022 cm−2 and Γ = 2.

Ponti et al. report information about each flaring block (denoted “B1” for the first block and so on) detected in their Bayesian block analysis. They report the quiescent block count rate by averaging over all the quiescent blocks of the observation under investigation and assume that the quiescent emission is constant within each observation. They report the 2-10 keV fluence in physical units of 10−10 erg cm−2, after subtraction of the average contribution of the quiescent blocks, but in this table we have computed the fluence by multiplying the 2-8 keV raw mean count rate by the flare duration to report the mean count rate in units of counts. Note that Ponti et al. use 0th order events only in gratings observations whereas the other authors used 0th and 1st order counts. All values are reported for NH = 15 × 1022 cm−2 and Γ = 2.

Neilsen et al. determine the flare duration, peak rate, and fluence from Gaussian fits to the X-ray light curve in 300 s bins and report these values as raw measurements not corrected for pileup. They report the pileup-corrected mean absorbed 2-8 keV flux and mean unabsorbed 2-10 keV luminosities of each flare estimated by scaling from the brightest flare and the results of Nowak et al. (2012).
